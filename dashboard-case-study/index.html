Case Study — Portfolio Operations

Portfolio Operations Dashboard & Tracking System

Built at Wintrust Financial Corporation to manage a $200M premium finance portfolio across 75–100 concurrent agency relationships · Jason Santos

Problem
Engagement health, onboarding progress, and renewal risk across 75-100 agencies lived in scattered manual review with no single view of where attention was actually needed.
Solution
Built a tracking system, starting in Excel and rebuilt in Power BI as the portfolio outgrew it, that consolidated activity signals, onboarding status, and renewal risk into one weekly view.
Result
Renewal outreach shifted from reactive to proactive, onboarding status became visible without asking, and a Sunday-night manual rebuild became a five-minute Monday check.
Why this matters: with no formal CS infrastructure and 75-100 concurrent relationships, the real constraint wasn't effort, it was attention allocation. Without a system to surface which accounts actually needed a call this week, time defaulted to whichever account was loudest, not whichever was most at risk.

Background

Same portfolio as the onboarding framework: 75-100 concurrent agency relationships, roughly $200M, one person, no dedicated CS tooling or ops function. Engagement tracking, onboarding status, and renewal pipeline were three separate problems I was solving by memory and manual review before this existed.

The Problem

Quote volume and transaction frequency were the leading indicators of an agency disengaging, but nothing surfaced them proactively — I'd notice a drop only when I happened to pull that agency's activity, usually because something else prompted me to look. Onboarding status for new agencies lived in a running spreadsheet I updated by hand, and renewal risk lived in whatever I remembered from the last conversation with that agency. Three separate tracking problems, none of them systematized, all competing for the same limited weekly attention.

Constraints

No engineering support and no dedicated analytics tooling budget going in — this had to be something I could build and maintain myself, on the side of full account-management responsibility for the book. No pre-existing view of which metrics actually predicted disengagement or renewal risk; the leading indicators (quote volume, transaction frequency trend) came from my own observation over time, not from a data team's model. And whatever I built had to keep working as the portfolio grew, which is exactly where the Excel version started to break.

My Approach

I started in Excel because it was the tool I had immediate access to: one tab per tracking dimension, manual pivot tables rebuilt weekly, scoring done by eye against quote volume and transaction counts. That was viable up to roughly 40-50 agencies. Past that point, the update itself started competing with the account work it was supposed to support — a Sunday-night rebuild that ate into the time it was meant to free up. The decision to rebuild in Power BI wasn't about wanting a nicer tool, it was that the manual version had crossed from "helpful" to "a second job."

Why It Worked — the Reasoning

The mechanism isn't "I built a dashboard" — it's that consolidating three separate tracking problems into one view changed what "attention" meant week to week. When engagement, onboarding, and renewal risk lived in three different places (memory, a spreadsheet, and nowhere), the accounts that got attention were whichever ones happened to surface — usually the loudest, not the most at-risk. A single consolidated view meant the accounts flagged for outreach were the ones the data actually pointed to, not the ones I happened to remember.

The renewal pipeline view is the clearest example: before, a renewal risk only became visible when activity had already been declining for a while and I happened to notice. After, a declining trend showed up as a flag before the renewal date, which meant the outreach conversation could happen while there was still time to change the outcome, not after the relationship had already cooled. The value wasn't the automation itself — it was that the automation moved the signal earlier in the timeline, from reactive to proactive.

Tradeoffs & Lessons Learned

What would I redesign today?

The activity thresholds that triggered a "watch" or "high risk" flag were set by eye, based on what looked concerning to me, not validated against actual churn outcomes. I never had enough downstream data to confirm whether a 3-month decline was actually predictive versus noise. I'd want a feedback loop that fed actual renewal/churn outcomes back into the thresholds, so the flags got more accurate over time instead of staying fixed at my initial judgment.

What data would I collect now that I couldn't then?

I was tracking quote volume and transaction frequency because those were the signals visible to me manually — I didn't have access to things like support ticket volume, feature adoption depth, or time-since-last-login, all of which are probably better churn predictors than raw activity counts. Today I'd want the dashboard pulling from more of the platform's actual usage data, not just the transaction layer.

Where's the limit of this design?

It's a single-operator tool — built for how I personally triaged the book, not designed to be handed to a team with shared definitions of what "at risk" means. Scaling it beyond one person would require turning my personal judgment calls into documented, agreed-upon criteria.

If I Rebuilt This in 2026

The Excel-to-Power BI shift solved the update-burden problem but didn't solve the threshold problem above — the flags were still my own judgment, encoded once. A few changes I'd make with what's available now:

  • LLM-assisted triage summaries. Instead of a rep scanning tables of flagged accounts, a weekly summary that explains in plain language why each flagged account is flagged — "declining transaction volume, 3rd month, similar pattern to two accounts that churned last year" — turns a data table into a decision aid.
  • Outcome-linked thresholds. Feed actual renewal outcomes back into the model so risk thresholds adjust based on what actually predicted churn, addressing the exact gap named in lessons learned above, instead of staying fixed at my original judgment calls.
  • Natural-language querying. Instead of building a fixed set of dashboard views in advance, letting a rep ask "which agencies in Tier 1 onboarding haven't logged in this week" directly, without needing a new report built for every new question.
  • Automated first-pass outreach drafts. For flagged renewal-risk accounts, a drafted outreach note referencing the specific activity trend, ready for a human to review and personalize rather than starting from a blank page — shrinking the gap between "flag surfaces" and "outreach happens."
